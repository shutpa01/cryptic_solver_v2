{# The Basics #}

The Basics — It's Not What You Think

Here's the thing nobody tells you: a cryptic crossword is no harder than a regular one. In a regular crossword, you get one definition and you have to think of the answer. In a cryptic, you get two ways to find the answer. That's not harder — that's easier.

A regular crossword clue:

"Guess" (8)

You just have to think of an 8-letter word meaning "guess". Could be ESTIMATE, SPECULATE... not easy!

The same clue, cryptic style:

"Guess teatimes varied" (8)

You still have the definition — "Guess". But now you also have instructions: "teatimes varied" tells you to rearrange the letters of TEATIMES. That gives you ESTIMATE. Two routes to the same answer.

Every cryptic clue has the same structure:

Always at the start or end Definition

A straight synonym, just like a regular crossword

+
The fun part Wordplay

Instructions to build the answer from pieces

Your job is simply to figure out where the definition ends and the wordplay begins. That's it. That's the whole game.

{# Understanding Wordplay #}

Understanding Wordplay

The wordplay part of a clue is built from fodder — words that contribute letters to the answer. Sometimes the fodder is just a straight synonym that gives you the answer or part of it:

"Old queen with place for trains" (8,7)

VICTORIA (old queen — Queen Victoria) + STATION (place — think fire station, coffee station) = VICTORIA STATION. "For trains" is the definition — it's a place for trains.

Two synonyms, side by side, building the answer. That's a charade.

Notice how "place" doesn't immediately scream STATION? That's typical. Cryptic setters love near-synonyms — words that are close enough to be fair but far enough to make you think. "Place" can mean station. "Old queen" can mean Victoria. Once you start seeing these loose connections, clues open up fast.

Often the fodder comes with an indicator — a word that tells you what to do with it:

"Trainee acted strangely" (5)

ACTED is the fodder. Strangely tells you to rearrange the letters. ACTED → CADET. And a cadet is a trainee — that's the definition.

That's an anagram. The indicator ("strangely") signals the rearrangement.

"Uncovers sleep over" (5)

SLEEP is the fodder. Over tells you to reverse it. SLEEP backwards → PEELS. And "uncovers" is the definition — to peel something is to uncover it.

That's a reversal. Another near-synonym: "uncovers" for PEELS.

"Rage found in danger" (5)

The answer is literally hiding inside the clue: dANGER. Found in is the indicator. And "rage" is the definition — anger is rage.

That's a hidden word — the easiest type to spot.

That's really all there is to it. Fodder gives you the letters, indicators tell you what to do with them, and the definition — always at the start or the end — confirms you've got the right answer. The rest is just variations on this theme.
